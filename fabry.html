Fabry Disease

Definition



Fabry Disease is a rare genetic disorder caused by the buildup of a particular type of fat,
called globotriaosylceramide (GL-3), in the body's cells. This buildup occurs due to a deficiency of the enzyme alpha-galactosidase A, which is responsible for breaking down GL-3.



Causes



Fabry Disease is caused by mutations in the GLA gene, which leads to the deficiency or absence of
the alpha-galactosidase A enzyme. It is inherited in an X-linked manner, meaning it primarily affects males, though females can also have symptoms.



Symptoms



Chronic pain, especially in the hands and feet

Skin rashes (angiokeratomas)

Decreased ability to sweat

Gastrointestinal issues

Kidney problems

Heart complications

Hearing loss

Treatment



While there is no cure for Fabry Disease, treatment focuses on managing symptoms and slowing disease progression.
Options include:



Enzyme replacement therapy (ERT)

Chaperone therapy

Medications for pain management

Dialysis or kidney transplantation for kidney failure

Heart medications to manage cardiac complications

Prevention



Since Fabry Disease is a genetic condition, it cannot be prevented. However, genetic counseling and testing can help
families understand the risks and consider early diagnosis and treatment options.
