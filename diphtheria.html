Diphtheria

Causes of Diphtheria



Diphtheria is caused by the bacterium *Corynebacterium diphtheriae*. The bacteria produce a toxin that affects the respiratory system,
and sometimes the skin and heart. The disease is highly contagious and can spread through:



Close contact with an infected person, such as through coughing or sneezing

Contact with contaminated surfaces or objects

Unhygienic conditions, particularly in overcrowded or unvaccinated populations

Symptoms of Diphtheria



The symptoms of diphtheria can vary from mild to severe. Common signs include:



Sore throat, fever, and fatigue

Thick, gray or white membrane in the throat or nose

Swollen glands (lymph nodes) in the neck

Difficulty swallowing or breathing

Skin lesions or ulcers (in case of cutaneous diphtheria)

Severe cases can lead to complications like heart failure, paralysis, and respiratory failure

Treatment for Diphtheria



Diphtheria is a medical emergency that requires immediate treatment. Treatment typically involves:



Antitoxin: A diphtheria antitoxin is administered to neutralize the toxin produced by the bacteria

Antibiotics: Antibiotics like penicillin or erythromycin are used to kill the bacteria and stop the infection from spreading

Supportive care: Oxygen therapy and mechanical ventilation may be needed in severe cases where breathing is compromised

Monitoring for complications: Cardiac monitoring and other supportive measures for possible heart and nerve involvement

Prevention of Diphtheria



The best way to prevent diphtheria is through vaccination. Other preventive measures include:



Diphtheria vaccine: The DTaP vaccine, which also protects against tetanus and pertussis, is given to children in a series of shots

Booster shots for adults, particularly if traveling to areas where diphtheria is more common

Practicing good hygiene, such as regular hand washing and avoiding close contact with infected individuals

Proper wound care and covering cuts or sores to prevent skin infections
