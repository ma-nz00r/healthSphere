Dengue Fever

Causes of Dengue



Dengue fever is caused by the dengue virus, which is transmitted to humans through the bite of infected female mosquitoes, primarily the *Aedes aegypti* mosquito. The virus can also be transmitted through:



Bites from infected mosquitoes, usually during daylight hours

In rare cases, from mother to child during pregnancy, childbirth, or breastfeeding

Blood transfusions or organ transplants (rare cases)

Symptoms of Dengue



The symptoms of dengue typically appear 4 to 10 days after being bitten by an infected mosquito. Common symptoms include:



High fever (up to 104°F or 40°C)

Severe headache and eye pain

Pain behind the eyes

Joint and muscle pain (often called "breakbone fever")

Nausea and vomiting

Fatigue and rash (often red and spotted)

In some severe cases, bleeding (nosebleeds, gum bleeding) and low platelet counts

Treatment for Dengue



Currently, there is no specific antiviral treatment for dengue. Treatment focuses on relieving symptoms and supporting the patient through recovery. Common treatments include:



Pain relief: Paracetamol (acetaminophen) is commonly used to manage pain and fever. Avoid NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen) due to the risk of bleeding.

Hydration: Fluid intake is crucial to prevent dehydration, especially in severe cases.

Hospitalization: In cases of severe dengue (dengue hemorrhagic fever or dengue shock syndrome), hospitalization and intravenous fluids may be necessary.

Monitoring: Close monitoring of blood pressure, platelet count, and organ function is essential for severe cases.

Prevention of Dengue



Preventing dengue involves reducing mosquito exposure and controlling mosquito populations. Some important measures include:



Eliminate mosquito breeding sites: Remove standing water in containers like buckets, tires, and flower pots where mosquitoes breed.

Wear protective clothing: Wear long-sleeved shirts, long pants, socks, and shoes to reduce mosquito exposure.

Use insect repellent: Apply insect repellents containing DEET on exposed skin.

Use mosquito nets: Sleep under a mosquito net, particularly in areas where dengue is prevalent.

Environmental control: Use insecticides and encourage community-wide efforts to eliminate mosquito breeding grounds.

Vaccination: In some areas, the dengue vaccine (Dengvaxia) is available for individuals aged 9–45 years who have been previously infected with the virus.
