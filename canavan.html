Canavan Disease

Causes of Canavan Disease



Canavan disease is caused by mutations in the

ASPA gene
which provides instructions for making an enzyme called aspartoacylase. This enzyme is responsible for breaking down a fatty
substance called N-acetylaspartic acid (NAA) in the brain. In individuals with Canavan disease, the enzyme is deficient or absent, leading to the accumulation of NAA, which causes brain damage.





Symptoms of Canavan Disease



Developmental delays

Loss of motor skills

Abnormal head enlargement (macrocephaly)

Muscle weakness and low muscle tone

Seizures

Vision problems

Treatment for Canavan Disease



Currently, there is no cure for Canavan disease. Treatment focuses on managing symptoms and improving quality of life. Some treatment approaches include:



Physical therapy to help with motor skills

Occupational therapy to promote daily living skills

Speech therapy to improve communication

Medications to control seizures

Regular monitoring of growth and development

Prevention of Canavan Disease



Canavan disease is inherited in an autosomal recessive manner, meaning both parents must carry a copy of the mutated gene for their child to be affected.
Genetic counseling and carrier screening can help identify individuals at risk. Prenatal testing can also be used to diagnose the disease before birth in some cases.
