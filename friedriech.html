Friedreich's Ataxia

What is Friedreich's Ataxia?



Friedreich's Ataxia (FA) is a genetic, neurodegenerative disorder that affects coordination, balance, and movement.
It is characterized by progressive damage to the nervous system, leading to impaired muscle control and speech difficulties.



Causes



Friedreich's Ataxia is caused by mutations in the FXN gene, which leads to a deficiency of the protein
frataxin. This protein is essential for the proper functioning of mitochondria, the energy-producing structures within cells.
The mutation results in the gradual destruction of nerve tissue in the spinal cord and peripheral nerves.





Symptoms



Progressive loss of muscle coordination (ataxia)

Muscle weakness, especially in the legs

Difficulties with speech and swallowing

Hearing loss

Vision problems (such as double vision)

Heart problems, including an enlarged heart

Treatment



There is currently no cure for Friedreich's Ataxia, but treatment focuses on managing symptoms and improving quality of life.
This may include:



Physical therapy to improve coordination and strength

Speech therapy for communication difficulties

Medication to manage heart complications

Supportive care for managing the progression of the disease

Prevention



Since Friedreich's Ataxia is an inherited condition, there is currently no known way to prevent it. Genetic counseling
is recommended for families with a history of the disease.
