Gallbladder Cancer

What is Gallbladder Cancer?



Gallbladder cancer is a rare type of cancer that begins in the gallbladder, a small organ beneath the liver that stores bile.
It is often difficult to detect in its early stages because it usually does not cause symptoms until it has progressed.
By the time it is diagnosed, gallbladder cancer may have spread to nearby tissues and organs.



Causes



The exact cause of gallbladder cancer is not known, but several factors may increase the risk of developing this type of cancer.
These include:



Age and gender: Gallbladder cancer is more common in people over the age of 70 and is more common in women than men.

Gallstones: The presence of gallstones, which can irritate the gallbladder, may increase the risk of cancer.

Chronic inflammation: Conditions that cause chronic inflammation of the gallbladder, such as chronic cholecystitis, may increase cancer risk.

Obesity: Being overweight or obese can increase the risk of gallbladder cancer.

Genetic factors: Family history and certain genetic mutations can increase the risk of gallbladder cancer.

Certain infections: Infection with the liver fluke parasite has been linked to an increased risk of gallbladder
cancer, especially in certain parts of the world.

Symptoms



Gallbladder cancer often does not cause symptoms in the early stages. However, as the cancer progresses, the following
symptoms may appear:

Abdominal pain, especially in the upper right side

Unexplained weight loss

Jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes)

Nausea and vomiting

Loss of appetite

Fever

Swelling in the abdomen

Treatment



Treatment for gallbladder cancer depends on the stage of the cancer, the overall health of the patient, and other factors.
Common treatment options include:



Surgery: The primary treatment for gallbladder cancer is surgical removal of the gallbladder, along with surrounding
tissue and lymph nodes if the cancer has spread.

Chemotherapy: Chemotherapy drugs are used to kill cancer cells or shrink tumors, either before or after surgery.

Radiation therapy: High-energy radiation may be used to target and kill cancer cells in certain cases.

Targeted therapy: Medications that target specific genes or proteins involved in the growth of cancer cells may be used in some cases.

Immunotherapy: New treatments are being explored that use the body's immune system to attack and destroy cancer cells.

Palliative care: In advanced stages, palliative care may be used to manage symptoms and improve quality of life.

Prevention



There is no guaranteed way to prevent gallbladder cancer, but certain steps may reduce the risk:



  • Maintaining a healthy weight through a balanced diet and regular physical activity

  • Avoiding obesity and managing weight-related health conditions

  • Seeking treatment for gallstones or chronic gallbladder inflammation

  • Avoiding excessive alcohol consumption

  • Consulting with a healthcare provider if you have a family history of gallbladder cancer
  • Being cautious about liver fluke infections in areas where the parasite is prevalent
