Gastric Cancer

What is Gastric Cancer?



Gastric cancer, also known as stomach cancer, is a type of cancer that begins in the lining of the stomach.
It occurs when abnormal cells in the stomach start to grow uncontrollably, forming a tumor.
Gastric cancer is often diagnosed at an advanced stage, making early detection crucial for effective treatment. It is more common in older adults,
especially those over the age of 60.



Causes



The exact cause of gastric cancer is not fully understood, but several factors may increase the risk of developing
the disease. Some known risk factors include:



Age and gender: Gastric cancer is more common in people over the age of 60, and men are more likely to develop the disease than women.

Helicobacter pylori infection: A bacterial infection in the stomach is a leading cause of gastric cancer.

Diet: A diet high in salty, smoked, or pickled foods may increase the risk of stomach cancer.

Smoking: Smoking tobacco can increase the risk of gastric cancer.

Family history: A family history of gastric cancer or genetic mutations may predispose individuals to the disease.

Chronic gastritis: Long-term inflammation of the stomach lining can increase the risk of gastric cancer.

Symptoms



The symptoms of gastric cancer can be subtle in the early stages but may become more noticeable as the disease progresses.
Common symptoms include:



Feeling full after eating small amounts of food

Indigestion or heartburn

Abdominal pain or discomfort

Nausea or vomiting

Unexplained weight loss

Loss of appetite

Treatment



Treatment for gastric cancer depends on the stage of the cancer, its location, and the patient's overall health.
Common treatment options include:



Surgery: The most common treatment for gastric cancer is surgery to remove the tumor or the entire stomach in some cases (gastrectomy).

Chemotherapy: Chemotherapy drugs are used to kill cancer cells or shrink tumors, and it may be used before or after surgery.

Radiation therapy: High-energy radiation is used to target cancer cells, often in combination with other treatments.

Targeted therapy: This treatment uses drugs that target specific molecules involved in cancer growth.

Immunotherapy: Newer treatments that stimulate the immune system to recognize and attack cancer cells may be used for advanced gastric cancer.

Prevention



While there is no guaranteed way to prevent gastric cancer, certain lifestyle changes and early detection
may help reduce the risk:



Quit smoking and avoid tobacco use. Eat a healthy diet with plenty of fruits, vegetables, and whole grains, while limiting the consumption of salty, smoked, and pickled foods.

Get screened for Helicobacter pylori infection, especially if you have a family history of gastric cancer.

Limit alcohol consumption, as excessive drinking can increase the risk of gastric cancer.

If you have chronic gastritis or other stomach conditions, seek treatment and regular check-ups with your healthcare provider.
